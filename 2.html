The Bunny Butcher

Act 1: The Hunter's Final Payment

TRI-COUNTY CHRONICLE

VOL. XCIV No. 114 — TUESDAY, OCTOBER 14, 1997
PRICE: $0.50

THE BUNNY BUTCHER STRIKES AGAIN!

Fourth Victim Found Flayed in Industrial District; Police Baffled by "Mascot Slasher"

BY ARTHUR PENDLETON

NEWARK: The city is gripping its children tighter today as the psychotic predator dubbed "The Bunny Butcher" by local tabloids has claimed a fourth victim.

Late last night, transit workers discovered the body of 24-year-old mechanic Thomas Miller behind the abandoned Sector 4 rail yard. Investigators confirm the signature, nightmarish hallmarks of the killer: Miller was dispatched with a weapon coroners describe as an "impossibly large, industrial-sized cleaver," before perfect, geometric patches of skin were systematically harvested from the torso.

Eyewitness accounts from a nearby watchman describe a surreal, towering silhouette fleeing into the midnight mist, a slender, form-fitting rabbit costume moving with an eerie, mechanical grace.

"I thought it was a sick prank," the anonymous watchman told reporters, his hands visibly shaking. "It looked like a mascot from a distance. But it was carrying a blade nearly as big as a grown man. It didn't run. It sort of... pirouetted into the shadows. Like it was taking a bow."

Police Chief Marcus Vance urged citizens to stay indoors after dark and report any suspicious behavior, particularly anyone matching the bizarre, patchwork description of the suspect.

"We are dealing with a deeply disturbed individual who treats these horrific acts as a theatrical performance," Vance stated in an emergency press conference. "We will find whoever is inside that suit."

The fog rolls over the industrial district tonight, it’s humid and a bit sticky, it would make anyone feel a little nauseous.

The ink on the newsprint is faded, the paper yellowed and crumpled.

I blink, drawing my tired eyes away from the yellowed clipping clamped to the old, beat-up clipboard in my hand. My fingers, marked by the tremor of age and obsession, trace the headline—a relic that reminds me of good times, hard times, and the primal thrill of the hunt. I have spent years following the trail of the Bunny Butcher, tracking the phantom through archives and shadows until the path finally led me here. The shine of my flashlight cuts through the heavy, damp darkness of the elevator shaft.

"It's gonna have to do," I say in a low voice.

I reach out, my trembling finger pressing the rusted call button of the industrial lift. For a long, breathless moment, nothing happens. Then, a violent, deep-chested shudder rattles the very foundation of the shaft. From the lightless depths below, the elevator begins its ascent—a labored, agonizing climb marked by the high-pitched squeak of unlubricated pulleys and the rhythmic, guttural groaning of strained cables. It sounds like a dying beast dragging itself toward the surface, every foot of progress a protest of ancient, oxidized iron.

The surface world thinks the Bunny Butcher disappeared decades ago, but I know better; I have followed the whispers and the blood until the trail went cold beneath the earth. I wrote that article nearly forty years ago.

As the heavy industrial elevator groans, plunging me deeper into the bowels of the earth. There is a sinking feeling that maybe deep down, I don’t really want to know. But it’s too late to turn back now.

Back to the end of the mind. To be forgotten, to shy away from the unbearable. Wanting to forget. When people forget about the realities of the past; The things that haunt us as a society is the nature of human kind. No matter how terrible the event, we always find some way to push forward and never look back, or at least most rather bury the past. Not me, I’ve never been one to bury the past; the past doesn’t need to be buried; it needs a champion. It demands to be seen, to be fed upon, to live through the one person brave enough to keep digging, to keep its scream alive within my own heart, no matter the cost to my mind.

The Bunny Butcher is a cruel being, notorious for using the flesh of others as fabric. No single victim of hers has ever survived her performances. They are always found cut up, skinned, and sometimes decapitated with missing heads for reasons unknown. She leaves a calling card at every scene horrific beyond belief, smelling like rotten fish and bleached pale white to catch the moonlight. This card stock, I know, was created from the very flesh of my fellow people. They are the ones I have devoted to serve, whose right to be known, to be followed up on, demands that I remember the past.

As I finish that last thought, the elevator doors shudder open with a terminal groan. I step cautiously out of the lift and into a short, sterile corridor. Several rusted metal signs hang from the ceiling, their text obscured by grime, but the one directly ahead is unmistakable: "SHOW ROOM." I follow the arrow, pushing through a set of heavy double doors that lead into a vast, desolate hall, a silent frozen subterranean museum of forgotten technology.

I take my first step, but the expected crunch of concrete never comes. Instead, the floor gives way with a sickening, wet squelch, replaced by a carpet of pale pink, pulsing flesh and a chaotic nest of tangled black wires that writhes under my weight. I look up, my breath hitching as I see the same macabre biological growth choking the walls, turning the hallway into the throat of some mechanical beast. I move forward with agonizing care, panning my heavy-duty flashlight across the vast, desolate hall of the "SHOW ROOM."

To my left, the beam reveals a defunct pizzeria alcove. Big Roni, the cartoon chef, stands there not as a mascot, but a nightmare. His once-white chef's hat is now a heavy, grime-stained gray, slumped over a face of rotting latex. Fleshy tentacles erupt from beneath his plastic mustache, and a single, bulging green eye stares at me. He has no legs, just a heavily rusted metallic pole rooted into the pulsing floor, the oxidation flaking off into the biomass.

I sweep the light further, catching the rabbit waitress in mid-tilt. Her tattered dress, once a vibrant uniform, now hangs in oily shreds off a frame where her right arm has been elongated into a multi-jointed claw. The mechanical components of her elbow are seized with thick, orange rust, studded with small, bloodshot eyeballs that seem to track my movement. I tilt my flashlight upward, the beam struggling to reach the heights of the cavernous ceiling where a massive, grimy sign flickers to life. The words "The Critters" emerge through the dust motes in faded, peeling paint, illuminating a grand three-stage configuration that spans the width of the hall.

Starting my survey on the far left, I find a forgotten, anthropomorphic red fox standing perfectly still in a solitary spotlight. He wears the face of a smug, self-satisfied bastard, a permanent grin and relaxed, half-lidded eyes. I know his type: the kind of man whose success is as stiff and frozen as his posture, preserved only by decades of abandonment. Even in decay, his formal, vintage-style three-piece suit,a dark red pinstriped blazer over a yellow vest remains arrogant, now covered in a thick, velvety layer of dust. The small, scruffy rat puppet in his grip looks utterly exhausted, a pathetic companion to such an arrogant, static display.

Stella Skunette remains locked in a performance pose, standing upright while clutching a grime-stained microphone. She still tries to seduce the empty room. Her once-pristine appearance is now a grotesque display of decay, featuring pink highlighted white spiky short hair and an hourglass figure. This is the truth of all idols: their beauty inevitably fails. I remember her from an old Vartti commercial, a slick, vibrant production that promised an impossible future. Even then, she was nothing more than a plastic lie, and now she is merely a perfect casualty of the inevitable rot. The patchy loss of her synthetic fur reveals a disturbing mixture of cracked white plastic, rusted mechanical parts, and raw, pulsing flesh, particularly evident underneath her arms.

Beside her, a rabbit guitarist sports a slicked-back haircut reminiscent of The Fonz. His black leather jacket is cracked and peeling over a white polo undershirt turned yellow with age. The costume material on his legs is ripped, revealing the pitted, rusted metal of his knees. He wears shades of blue, oversized, rounded, thick-framed retro sunglasses.

I do believe those are a type of Pit Viper. I recall having a pair like that as a kid. A magnificent pair. Such a glorious memory... but the rabbit doesn't deserve them, no. It can't appreciate the speed! It can't see the light!

The rabbit holds what appears to be a six-string guitar, but upon closer inspection, it has an unusual addition of nineteen piano keys modified onto it.

Across the left side from him sits a dazed alligator who appears significantly chubby and bottom-heavy, giving the distinct impression that she hasn't moved from her sitting position in years. Her mottled pink-green skin, the color of spoiled, sickly plastic, is exactly what I came to see the past exposed and screaming. Her costume is coated in a thick layer of sticky grime, half-consumed by the encroaching rot. She is in a laid-back drummer pose at a drum set. Her green eyes with red sclera stare blankly ahead, dressed in a pink tank top that barely stretches over her large belly.

Finally, the beam finds the far right stage, where an old country anthropomorphic dog is slumped over on the piano. He is the oldest and is in the worst state of repair, appearing almost falling apart with a spine that looks broken. This is a true victim. The worn, one-piece blue trouser and synthetic fur ripped and matted it looks like he was almost skinned alive and beaten nearly to death as he sits head down laying upon the keys of an old-looking piano. I can appreciate this tableau of agony; it’s an honest representation of the end game, a warning of the pain required to truly honor the past.

The stench, a thick combination of brine, rot, and ozone, had finally breached my defenses. This pit of hell demanded better filtration. My fingers nervously dug into the expensive silk lining of my black coat, retrieving the worn black bandana. I positioned the cloth squarely over my nose and mouth, a gesture more for ritual than actual protection. The smell would fall an ordinary man—perhaps kill them. But I am no ordinary man; I am the great-grandson of Spencer Pendleton.

My scent alone might even be a useful weapon. I couldn't recall my last proper wash, but such trivialities didn't matter. Not now. The task at hand, the hunt, was everything. I forced myself to move deeper into the rot, the silence pressing down on me.

A loud, metal-on-metal scraping sound echoes from the right side of the hall, near the defunct arcade and prize area. Driven by a primal journalistic instinct to finally finish the hunt, I click off my flashlight and secure it to my toolbelt. Tucking my clipboard tightly under my left armpit, I reach over my shoulder to draw my trusty 1873 Winchester rifle from my back. I move cautiously toward the noise, my fingers white-knuckled around the cold iron.

"WHERE ARE YOU HIDING, BUNNY? TIME TO SETTLE ACCOUNTS! I'VE COME TO COLLECT!"

I shout with manic, excited gusto, my voice echoing violently in the vast, dead silence. A large grin appeared on my face, expecting some kind of challenge or acknowledgment in return. Only the soft creaking of warped, strained metal answers me. I swing my rifle forward and edge around the corner.

My flashlight beam slices through the gloom, revealing a massive room crammed with old, rusted-out arcade cabinets. In the center, slumped tragically on a plastic bench, sits a black and white cat animatronic. It looks dead and tired, its jaw hanging open in a silent scream, and its joints sagging under the weight of decades. It’s wearing a faded, brightly colored uniform, its cracked plastic shell worn smooth by time, a silent monument to this forgotten place.

I look down and realize the sickening, wet squelch is finally gone. My heavy boots strike the soft surface of classic '90s arcade carpet, a stark and welcome contrast to the pulsing pink flesh I just left behind. A wave of profound relief washes over me, potent enough to nearly buckle my stiff knees as I breathe in the stale but comparatively clean air of the arcade. It is in this momentary reprieve that I notice a faint, almost ethereal green light emitting from the area over the prize counter.

I move toward it, but the path is cluttered; I have to shove aside fallen displays and a mess of trash that has spilled across the floor before I can reach the counter itself.

I grunt, the weight of my years and the stiffness of my coat making every movement a chore as I heave myself over the dusty prize counter. My boots hit the floor on the other side with a muffled thud, and I move toward the terminal, eager to see what secrets this ancient machine still holds. The old PC terminal screen flickers with a persistent, low green glow, its glass surface layered with decades of grime and fine fractures that distort the text. I put my hand on it and wipe the screen to make it easier to see.

To the side of the prize counter, a promotional cardboard cutout had fallen over. It was a blocky, cheery smiled animatronic with a distinctly feminine shape, large hands, and cuffs. A small top hat sat on her head, framing a cute short haircut, and her eyes looked like they could hypnotize me. The figure’s body, a molded shell of pristine plastic with a uniform printed directly onto the curves of its frame, possessed a sickeningly cute design that made my stomach churn.

I tore my gaze away, a hot flush of embarrassment prickling the back of my neck. She was a ghost from my own history, a plastic echo of the woman at the Pendleton event the night my legacy rotted. That yearly ritual, birthed by the cold ambition of my great-grandfather Spencer, was supposed to be my baptism in blood. Instead, it was my public execution. I can still feel the weight of the rifle, a leaden anchor in my small, eight-year-old hands, as I stared down the sights at a girl much older than I was. She had pale, porcelain skin and rosy cheeks that flushed with terror, her short hair matted with sweat.

My finger had curled around the trigger, but my entire soul had locked. I was a coward, a pathetic stain on the Pendleton name, and the laughter of my kin still rings in my ears like a physical blow. No more. I am not the embarrassment they whispered about in the dining room. I will carve my own legend from this rot. I can do this; I will do it; I MUST do it!

I turn my eyes back to the screen, my vision blurring with a desperate, frustrated flushed of heat my face:

Date: 02/02/1987

From: CEO, Vartti Enterprises

To: All Staff

Subject: A Bright New Horizon for Entertainment!

Hello Team! I am absolutely thrilled to share some fantastic news with you all.

We are officially confirming the formal merger of Vartti, our beloved toy company, which recently acquired government research funds to improve the future and Locke Robotics development infrastructures into a single, unified company!

This incredible partnership is the key to streamlining our facility-wide updates and resource management, ensuring we stay at the cutting edge of the industry. Together, we aren't just making toys or robots; we are building the very future of theatrical entertainment! Let's keep this momentum going as we dazzle the world together!

My hands, slick with the cold sweat of anticipation, tremble violently as I hammer the back arrow key. I navigate the archaic system with a desperate, frantic energy, my heart hammering against my ribs like a trapped bird. I need those higher-level records; I need the truth of the Butcher’s genesis. But the screen suddenly fractures into a strobe of crimson light, a stark, mocking prompt bleeding through the grime:

"SECURE ACCESS REQUIRED. PLEASE ENTER ADMINISTRATIVE PASSWORD."

A guttural curse escapes my parched throat. I pivot from the mocking glow, noticing the heavy, reinforced backroom door tucked behind the counter, the only threshold in this tomb still sealed. I immediately begin a frantic, clawing search for the key beneath the counter where I am standing, my fingers raking through the thick layers of filth and history. Finally, deep in the suffocating shadows beneath the lip of the countertop, my skin brushes against a biting chill. I yank it free from a single heavy iron key dangling from a rusted hook. I don't hesitate; I jam the jagged metal into the lock, and with a sharp, bone-deep click, the bolt surrenders. The door creaks open, exhaling the stale, frozen breath of the dark.

The interior of the prize storage room is a claustrophobic graveyard of forgotten celebrations. Stacks of cardboard boxes, damp and collapsing under their own weight, spill a colorful but decayed contents of outdated plushies with matted fur and missing button eyes. Rusted helium tanks stand like silent sentinels in the corners, surrounded by the shriveled, leathery husks of deflated balloons that cling to the floor like shed skins. Most prominent among the wreckage are piles of rubber balls that have lost their bounce, now pitted and dull, and a tangle of neon-colored sticky hands that have devolved into a goopy, translucent sludge, adhering to everything they touch with a sickening, chemical grip.

Tucked away behind a stack of rotting boxes, I spot a heavy, unmarked metal door with the faded, hand-painted sign: "REPAIR & DIAGNOSTICS." The door is cleverly concealed by the shadows and debris, serving as a hidden service pathway that looks to loop around behind the main stage area, leading directly to the heart of the facility where all the complex equipment for servicing the animatronics is located.

As I push through the door, and the narrow hallway ahead seems to stretch unnaturally, the perspective warping until the far end is a pinprick of light in a sea of shadow. Every step I take feels as if the air itself is thickening into a heavy, invisible sludge that clings to my coat and pulls at my boots. My lungs burn with the effort of simply moving forward, the silence of the corridor becoming a physical weight that presses against my eardrums with agonizing intensity. With each passing yard, the walls feel closer, the ceiling lower, until the very act of lifting my rifle becomes a monumental struggle against an unseen gravity that wants to anchor me to this rot forever.

Reaching upon the end of the hallway I can finally take a much needed deep breath, something is wrong, but I quite can’t figure it out, it’s getting tight in my chest. Am sweating like a damn pig as if I am roasting in an oven like a chicken dinner at midnight.

I push into the room, and the air here is different—sharper, biting with the scent of ozone and old, congealed blood. My flashlight beam dances over a chaotic graveyard of maintenance: screwdrivers of every size lie scattered like discarded bones, and wrenches are piled in rusted heaps alongside complex diagnostic machines that blink with a dying, rhythmic light.

But it is the center of the room that stops my breath. Two heavy strap tables sit there, bolted to the floor, their leather restraints dark and stiffened. Seeing them reminds me of a bizarre report I heard a few years ago regarding the theft of two similar bolted strap tables from a local clinic.

I draw my Winchester rifle from my back, the cold weight of the iron steadying my nerves as I hold it ready in my trembling hands. I take one step, then two. A weird metal-metal screeching followed by a wet, rhythmic breathing sound of a soft hiss-squelch catches my attention, not from ahead, but right behind me.

Before I could fully turn, a crushing, iron weight slams into the back of my head. The massive, industrial knife connects with a sickening thunk, and my vision blurs instantly. My head pounds against the metal floor, leaving me dazed and unable to comprehend the attack.

I am face-down against the freezing, damp metal floor, the metallic tang of blood filling my mouth. My breath comes in ragged, wet gasps as I struggle to process the sudden, crushing impact to the back of my head. The pain is a white-hot pulse, threatening to pull me into unconsciousness, but the sheer, primal adrenaline of the hunt keeps my eyes wide, staring at the blurred rivets of the metal reinforced backstage floor.

My limbs feel like leaden anchors, unresponsive and heavy. With a desperate, agonizing grunt that tears at my parched throat, I begin the torturous process of rolling over. It takes every shred of my waning will and a brutal, elbow-scraping heave to shift my weight. My spine protests with every inch of movement, a thousand years of punishment compressed into a single, clumsy rotation until I finally fall onto my back, gasping for air as I look upward to confront the nightmare that stands over me.

My trusty Winchester rifle lies a few feet away, uselessly flung out of my grasp by the impact. My signature hat had been ripped from my head, now resting on the metal floor near my boots. It is a tall, red RCA-style hat, its crown defined by a sharp mule kick in the front and a subtle devil's tail in the back. The gambler-style brim features a deep pencil curl and a heavy pull-down. It is adorned with an implied skull, a coiled black snake, a wrap of rusted barbed wire, and a signature gold nail. This hat was a gift from my great grandfather after I hunted 25 people; Now, it sits as a silent casualty of the attack.

Clank. Squelch.

The noise hit again, that terrible metal-to-metal screeching. This time, the sound didn't just scrape the floor; it was a painful, high-frequency blast that pierced the ringing in my ears, driving white-hot agony through the back of my skull.

I didn’t notice at first but she was already nearly on top of me. From down here, she looks impossibly tall. The form-fitting bunny suit is stretched tight over a slender, rigid frame, its texture a macabre patchwork of mismatched skin tones, pale cream sewn violently into deep, rotting browns. Where the seams stretch too far, you can see the sickening twitch of raw, invasive facility biomass pulsing underneath the fabric.

She tilts her oversized, stitched rabbit mask with a sickening, mechanical jerk, and a hollow, cute female voice a jagged fragment of a human thought echoes from the suit:

"Standard protocol... just a quick check..."

With an agonizingly slow, theatrical grace, she brings her right arm forward. Her hand is fused to a rusted, industrial slab of iron, a kitchen cleaver so massive it looks like it was ripped straight from a factory machine. She drags the cleaver across the concrete, the rusted metal screeching and scraping against the ground in a terrible protest. Despite the immense weight, she pivots the blade over in a labored, forced rotation, like a perverse performer struggling with an oversized prop.

She steps closer. The padded, clawless foot of her suit pressed right against the edge of the discarded newspaper not so far from me.

The giant blade rises, blocking out the dim light above. She doesn't rush. She tilts her head, waiting for you to look her right in those unblinking, stitched eyes. She says, the words a low, chilling whisper that seems to vibrate from the suit itself:

"Don't worry, you will become—useful."

The massive, industrial blade descends, but it doesn't deliver the mercy of a quick death. Instead, the edge bites into my shoulder with a cold, agonizing precision, pinned to the floor by her immense weight. I try to scream, but the sound is a wet, gurgling choke as the first strip of my own skin is peeled away. The pain is absolute a white-hot, screaming electricity that sets every nerve ending in my body on fire.

I watch with a detached, sickening horror as she works, her movements fluid and rhythmic, like a seamstress at a loom. I am no longer the hunter, no longer the champion of the past; I am merely raw material, a collection of geometric patches being harvested for a new performance. Each pull of the blade is a fresh descent into hell, the air hitting my exposed muscles like a thousand needles. As the last of my strength ebbs away, I realize with a final, crushing clarity that I have finally found the Bunny Butcher, and my payment for the hunt is to become part of her. The edges of the room begin to fray, the green glow of the monitors fading into a merciful, heavy void.

* * *

Everything went black for Arthur Pendleton.

The rhythmic, wet squelch of the facility echoed through the repair room as the biological organism began its final harvest. Arthur's broken body was hoisted onto one of the bolted strap tables. Across from him, the Bunny Butcher stood over a pristine custom-built mechanical chassis, its silver internals gleaming under the flickering lights. She signaled the Mother to initiate the final phase.

The organism surged, thick red veins erupting from the floor to envelop both the man and the machine. Arthur’s matter was systematically broken down and poured into the mechanical frame like molten lead. The Bunny Butcher worked with a seamstress’s precision, taking the geometric patches of harvested skin and stitching them directly onto the new, revived form.

As the last needle pierced the dermis, the entity on the table twitched with a violent, electric life. A new killer emerged from the rot, his eyes snapping open to reveal a cold, predatory intelligence. His face was a mask of patchwork history, featuring a brutal, squarish jawline adorned with long, thick mutton chops crafted from the matted remains of his own former hair.

He rose from the table with a mechanical groan. The hunt had not ended; the tradition had evolved. The Mutton Chop Devil has been born; no longer the Arthur Montgomery Pendleton we knew.

EPILOGUE: The First Offering

The midnight air of Newark was a biting caress against the patchwork of Arthur Montgomery Pendleton’s new visage, yet he no longer perceived the elements with the frail sensitivity of a man. Beneath the expensive, dirt-stained black silk coat the same shroud he wore when he was merely a hunter of flesh. There lays a formidable chassis of cold steel and pulsing, bio-organic mass. He was a masterpiece of the Bunny Butcher’s design, his mechanical frame reinforced by the invasive biomass of the facility below. He moved with a sharp, rhythmic squelch, a sound that should have been clumsy but instead possessed a terrifying, predatory efficiency. His mutton chops, matted with the grime of his transformation, twitched with every mechanical rotation of his head. He was no longer the disgraced journalist; he was the First Offering, the legacy of Spencer Pendleton reborn into a twisted form that could finally fulfill the family’s dark ritual without the weakness of a human heart.

The city was a labyrinth of shadows and neon, a hunting ground that felt both familiar and alien. As he stalked through the industrial district, the weight of the antique Winchester rifle felt like a natural extension of his new limbs. He wasn't looking for a story anymore. He was looking for a tribute, driven by a primal, almost lustful thrill for the chase that set his metallic frame humming with a dark heat.

He deliberately slowed his movements, allowing the rhythmic, heavy thud of his boots to reach the drifter's ears, savoring the way the boy’s pace quickened in terror. He descended from the heights with an agility that defied his bulky, patchwork frame, his boots hitting the pavement with a muffled thud as the biomass within them absorbed the shock. The drifter turned, eyes widening at the sight of the towering figure in the silk coat and the tall red hat. Arthur didn't move at first; he simply stood in the shadows, his presence a heavy, looming weight. The silence was broken by the rhythmic, labored wheeze of his biological lungs. A deep, metallic rasp that seemed to rattle the very air. Between each heavy breath, a wet, rhythmic smacking of his stitched lips echoed through the industrial gloom, a sound of visceral, predatory hunger. A wide, jagged grin split his face, and he began to close the distance with a slow, deliberate step. The drifter backed away, paralyzed by the sheer, inevitable gravity of the nightmare before him, until there was nowhere left to run.

Arthur leaned in, the emerald phosphor of his eyes reflecting the man's final, silent prayer. "Better run boy!" he said, the words surfacing in a gravelly, half-laughing that vibrated through his reinforced chest with each word. A moment of silence with no motion to the frightened drifter, then in a raspy voice, "Run!"

He reached for his belt, where his field kit hung like a butcher's trophy rack. On the right side sat a massive hunting knife, its serrated edge caked in dried, blackened ichor. Carried on the left side was the rest of his specialized harvesting kit; prominent among the surgical tools was a fluorescent orange Butt Out tool, its purpose for dressing the largest of prey rendered sickeningly clear by the geometric scars on Arthur's own patchwork frame. The hunt reached its inescapable conclusion, signaled by a gravelly cynical laugh that tore through the night, a multi-tonal cackle that sounded like rasping metal and wet, tearing fabric, abruptly cutting off into the heavy, definitive gunshot of an echoing gun piercing the gloomy sky.
